## Purpose

This document is the plan-of-record for two new sub-sections of `/docs/design/` on the Clef language site. Both consolidate material that is currently distributed across blog posts, internals walkthroughs, the categorical-foundations sub-section, and the four arXiv preprints. Neither is the canonical statement of the architecture today. The plan creates the canonical statements and assigns the work to specific files.

The two sub-sections are intentionally orthogonal. The first, `/docs/design/proof-architecture/`, treats verification at the level of decidability fragments and proof-pattern composition. It is invariant across substrates. The second, `/docs/design/substrate-tcb/`, treats the trust dependencies introduced by each compilation target. It is invariant across tiers.

A summary blog post on the SpeakEZ site announces both sub-sections once they are complete and provides the prose entry point for enterprise readers. The summary is short. The sub-sections are the canonical material.

## Why Two Sub-Sections, Not One

The four-tier proof architecture and the substrate TCB profile are two orthogonal axes of the verification story. A single document covering both would conflate dimensions that change for different reasons. The tiers change as a function of the decidability fragment the obligation lives in. The TCB profile changes as a function of which downstream toolchain and runtime the binary is delivered through. A reader asking "what kind of property can I verify" needs the proof-architecture material. A reader asking "what do I have to trust to deploy on substrate X" needs the substrate-tcb material. The two readers are distinct, and the documents should be too.

There is one place the axes intersect: the Tier 4 documentation must note that the Rocq foundational lemma library is part of the TCB at Tier 4 and not at Tiers 1 through 3. This is a single sentence in the Tier 4 document with a cross-link to `substrate-tcb/_index.md`. The intersection does not merit a third sub-section.

## Calibration: Where the Design Documents Sit

The design documents are calibrated to a specific layer of the documentation stack and should not drift from that layer in either direction. They are not theoretical framing in the categorical-foundations sense; that material already exists in `/docs/design/categorical-foundations/` and the design documents cross-reference it without duplicating it. They are not implementation mechanics at the source-file-and-function-call level; that material exists in `/docs/internals/pipeline/`, `/docs/internals/verification/`, and `/docs/internals/mlir/`, with the deeper specification grain captured in `clef-lang-spec`. The design documents sit between these layers and carry what can be called architectural mechanics: more than theory because they explain how proofs are carried through compilation and how certificates are emitted, less than implementation because they do not document compiler internals at the level a contributor would need to modify the relevant code.

This calibration is the structural reason the design documents are needed. The categorical-foundations material gives the categorical reading. The internals and spec material gives the implementation. Neither tells the story of how the architectural commitment becomes operational. The design documents are that story, told at architectural depth.

The existing blog posts (`proofs-from-dimensional-types.md`, `proofs-for-the-real-world.md`, `doubling-down-dmm-dts.md`, `cryptographic-certainty.md`, `runtime-revolution-fidelity.md`, `the-return-of-the-compiler.md`, `categorical-deep-learning.md`, `a-triangle-without-mystery.md`, and the forthcoming "Contour of the Flat Pointer") highlight the architecture in narrative form. They are motivating prose, not canonical reference. The design documents are written to distill what these posts gesture at and to make the mechanics explicit. Each design document cites the relevant blog posts as material to the process, not as the canonical statement. This division keeps the prose accessible and the canonical reference precise, which is the organization the rest of the documentation already follows.

Each tier and substrate document covers four mechanics at this depth:

**Witness annotation lifecycle**: how a proof obligation enters the PSG as a witness, how saturation refines it, what attribute it becomes at MLIR lowering, and how it is preserved through subsequent dialect transformations down to the residual evidence in the binary.

**Re-discharge cadence**: where in the pipeline the framework's chosen SMT solver (under the same-solver discipline) is invoked, what fragment the obligation lives in at each invocation, and what the response-time profile is at design time and compile time. The solver is at design-time and compile-time only; verification-time invokes only Rocq.

**Certificate emission**: what the verification certificate contains for an obligation in the relevant tier, how the global section is constructed from the per-stage stalk values along the compilation poset, how the certificate references the lemma library when applicable, and how the dual-pass agreement is recorded.

**Reconciliation reach**: how far the verification chain extends past the certificate (binary, IPC boundary, network edge via BAREWire), and where the chain terminates relative to the substrate's TCB. This is the dimension along which the proof-architecture and substrate-tcb sub-sections meet.

Each of these mechanics is covered at architectural depth in the design document. Granular detail lives in the internals sub-sections; specification grammar lives in `clef-lang-spec`. The design document is the place where a careful reader learns what the framework actually does, with cross-links that take them deeper when they need depth.

## Voice and Posture

This planning document and the design documents it scaffolds describe a forward-looking architecture. The capabilities they articulate are planned, not extant. The voice across all of these documents must reflect this distinction explicitly, because the topic area demands precision and a present-tense indicative voice would imply current capability where there is none. Three rules apply.

The first rule is to use forward-looking or architecturally neutral voice for capabilities that do not yet exist in the framework's codebase or distribution. "Will," "is intended to," "is planned to," "is to be implemented as," and analogous constructions are appropriate forward-looking forms. Architecturally neutral declarative forms (such as "the framework's compilation pipeline emits X" used to describe the planned architecture, with surrounding context making the planning posture explicit) are also appropriate. Present-tense indicative forms that imply the capability is currently shipping ("the framework produces X," "the verifier runs Y") are not appropriate when X or Y does not yet exist.

The second rule is to reserve present-tense indicative voice for capabilities that currently exist in the framework's repositories or in published artifacts. The existing arXiv preprints (`arXiv:2603.16437`, `arXiv:2603.17627`, `arXiv:2603.18104`, `arXiv:2603.25414`), the existing blog posts on the Clef site and the SpeakEZ site, the existing internals documents at `/docs/internals/`, the existing categorical-foundations documents at `/docs/design/categorical-foundations/`, the existing repositories (Composer at its current implementation state, Clef parsing as currently implemented, the compilation pipeline as it currently runs), and existing reference deployments (HelloArty as a working FPGA artifact) can be referred to in present tense because they exist. Forward extensions of these capabilities are described in forward-looking or architecturally neutral voice.

The third rule applies to the design documents the planning document scaffolds. Each tier document, substrate document, the certificate-mechanics document, the proof-architecture `_index.md`, and the substrate-tcb `_index.md` will use forward-looking or architecturally neutral voice for the planned architecture and present-tense voice for cross-references to existing material. The boundary between planned and extant must be visible to a reader. A reader landing on any of these documents should come away with two clear understandings: what the architecture is (described in architecturally neutral or forward-looking terms) and what currently exists in the codebase versus what is planned (with the boundary visible). Documents that fail this discipline must be revised before publication.

The discipline applies to the planning document itself. Where the planning document describes the planned architecture (the four-tier regime, the cross-cutting architectural commitments, the Verif/SMT dialect integration, the certificate format, the verification workflow, the substrate-tcb sub-section), the voice is architecturally neutral or forward-looking. Where the planning document references existing material (the arXiv preprints, the existing blog posts, the existing internals documents, the existing repositories), the voice is present-tense because those artifacts exist. The planning document is the first document to live by this rule and is the model the downstream design documents follow.

## Sub-Section One: Proof Architecture

Path: `/docs/design/proof-architecture/`.

### Tier 1 as a Regime of Decidable Theories

Tier 1 is not a single proof pattern and is not even a single solver fragment. It is a regime of structural proof obligations unified by three properties: decidability, automaticity, and composability through PSG saturation. Within this regime the framework uses several distinct algebraic theories that should not be conflated in the documentation. Each theory has its own algebraic substrate, its own proof pattern, and its own free-theorem story; what they share is the regime's defining cost profile (no developer annotation, polynomial-time inference, IDE-time response-time guarantee).

The currently identified Tier 1 theories are:

The **abelian-group algebra over `Z^n`** carries dimensional information. The free generators are the base dimensions (length, time, mass, temperature, electric current, luminous intensity, amount of substance, plus user-defined non-numeric units). Dimensional consistency reduces to linear equations over the integers, decided by Gaussian elimination in polynomial time and discharged by the framework's chosen SMT solver via QF_LIA at compile time and by Coq's `lia` at verification time. Wadler-Reynolds parametricity over the dimensional metadata gives the free theorem that dimensional consistency is preserved through dimension-polymorphic functions. This is the substrate documented in `decidability-sweet-spot.md` and the DTS-DMM paper.

The **finite-lattice theory for flat pointer state sets** carries representation information at the reference layer. The state space is closed and finite (nil, valid, forwarded, tagged with a compile-time constant tag, and three thunk states for unevaluated suspensions, black-hole markers, and indirection nodes). The lattice has fixed finite height bounded by the cardinality of the state space, so the saturation fixed point is computed in time linear in the PSG size. Lattice intersection is the saturation operation; lattice inclusion at use sites is constant time. Reynolds parametricity over the state-set type variable gives the free theorem that a function polymorphic over the admissible state set cannot dereference, because dereferencing requires a specific state and the function does not know which states are in the set. The compile-time discharge uses a small QF_BV encoding under the framework's chosen SMT solver; the verification-time discharge uses Coq's finite-lattice procedures from the prelude. The proof pattern is parametric, not constraint-solving. The forthcoming draft article "The Contour of the Flat Pointer" (Houston Haynes, draft dated 2026-04-21) is the canonical statement of this theory and should be linked from `tier-1-flat-pointer.md` when it is published. The article also positions the flat pointer alongside `Option<T>` as complementary constructs operating at different layers (representation versus logical alternative), which is essential framing the Tier 1 documentation must preserve.

The **Cayley structure of geometric algebra** carries grade information for Clifford-algebra workloads. Grade is treated as a dimension axis within the broader DTS abelian-group framework, and grade preservation through arithmetic operations follows from parametricity over the grade variable. The structural zeros of the Cayley table are eliminated at compile time, which is the basis for the categorical-deep-learning architectural treatment. The decision procedure is Gaussian elimination on the grade vector plus Cayley-table lookup; both are polynomial in the dimension of the geometric algebra.

The **QF_BV fragment for numeric representation** carries the choice of word width, accumulation discipline, and bit-level invariants for numeric types. Posit width selection, IEEE-754 placement, fixed-point selection, and quire accumulation discipline live here. The decision procedure is bit-blasting plus SAT, sound and complete on QF_BV in time bounded by the bit width, executed at compile time by the framework's chosen SMT solver and at verification time by the prelude's structural Coq tactics. The QF_BV fragment also underlies the compile-time encoding of the flat pointer state lattice when SMT-level discharge is required, but the proof patterns at the two theories are distinct: the flat pointer theory is parametric, while the numeric-representation theory is direct constraint-solving over fixed-width bitvectors.

These four theories are distinct algebraic substrates. They share Tier 1 because they admit fully-automatic, polynomial-time-bounded inference and discharge with no annotation cost in the engineer's normal workflow. They cohere not by reducing to a single logic but by riding parallel fibers over a shared emergent hypergraph; this is the connective-tissue architecture the next section formalizes.

### How the Tier 1 Theories Cohere

Tier 1's coherence is architectural, not logical. The theories share a base structure (the emergent hypergraph) and ride on it as parallel fibers, with each theory's witnesses, refinement rule, and decision procedure operating independently of the others. The proof-architecture `_index.md` document canonizes this connective tissue. The forthcoming "Fabric of Tier 1" blog post (Houston Haynes, draft dated 2026-04-21, not yet published) is the prose source and should be cross-linked as the narrative entry point, mirroring the relationship between "Contour of the Flat Pointer" and `tier-1-flat-pointer.md`.

The `_index.md` covers the connective tissue in five named architectural concepts.

**The emergent hypergraph.** The PHG is not imposed top-down. It is built up as elaboration proceeds: each operation's typing rule determines the arity and shape of the hyperedge that represents it. A binary arithmetic operation produces a 2-source hyperedge; a k-simplex join produces a (k+1)-source hyperedge; a tile co-location constraint produces a hyperedge whose source set is the full set of co-located nodes. The hypergraph grows alongside the PSG, with each hyperedge's arity determined by the operation's semantic requirements. The compiler does not pattern-match programs into a fixed graph structure; the graph structure is a consequence of what the program contains.

**Parallel fibers.** Each Tier 1 theory contributes a fiber over the hypergraph: the dimensional fiber holds the dimensional witness at every node, the state fiber holds the flat pointer state witness at every node, the grade fiber holds the grade witness, the coeffect fiber holds the lifetime witness, and any future Tier 1 theory adds its own fiber. Refinements propagate along hyperedges in each theory's own terms, with each pass touching only its own witness. Saturation is the joint completion of all passes, bounded by the slowest individual theory's termination because the passes do not block each other. Adding a new Tier 1 theory means adding a new fiber and refinement pass; the existing theories remain unaware of it and it remains unaware of them.

**Sheaf coherence at transfer boundaries.** Each theory has its own sheaf structure over the PHG, with its own admissible local choices at nodes and its own agreement condition on hyperedges. Saturation computes the global section of each sheaf in parallel during elaboration. When one sheaf fails to admit a global section, the diagnostic Lattice surfaces points to the specific theory and the specific hyperedge where coherence failed, not to a generic proof failure. The sharpest form of this is at transfer boundaries, where BAREWire carries values across processes, network edges, or heterogeneous targets. The compiler represents each transfer as a hyperedge annotated with the BAREWire schema, and each theory's sheaf checks its own coherence condition against the shared schema. The dimensional sheaf verifies units, the representation sheaf verifies fidelity bounds, the state sheaf verifies pointer-transport mode. The transfer's global section is the joint assignment satisfying all conditions against the schema simultaneously, and a failure is surfaced at the source line where the boundary crossing occurs, with the responsible clause of the schema named in the diagnostic. This is also the point at which the proof-architecture sub-section meets the substrate-tcb sub-section: BAREWire transfers are where reconciliation reach extends past a single substrate's TCB.

**The fibered zipper.** Navigation through the PHG uses a zipper in the Huet-McBride sense, parametric in hyperedge arity. The base zipper traverses the PHG's structural edges and carries a context recording the path from root to focus. At each focus, the zipper observes the node's witnesses, which are fibers over the base. The base navigation induces simultaneous movement in every fiber. Adding a new Tier 1 theory means adding a new fiber; the base zipper does not change, the navigation machinery does not change, and the new fiber travels along the same hyperedges with the existing ones. The zipper's slot count at the focus depends on the hyperedge's arity, and the fibers ride on whatever context the base presents.

**The adjoint correspondence and the dual-pass architecture.** The dual-pass is two distinct kinds of work performed on the same PHG with the same zipper: a computation pass that emits MLIR conditioned on the witnesses at each node, and a proof pass that discharges the Tier 1 obligations on every hyperedge. Both passes observe the same fibers. The MLIR emitted by the computation pass is conditioned on the same witnesses the proof pass certifies, which is the consistency property that makes the dual-pass a witness for the adjoint correspondence at every edge of the compilation poset. Code whose correctness depends on a missing certificate is not emitted. Each theory's certificate is discharged in its own polynomial-time terms, in parallel with the others.

**Demonstrable equivalence.** The structural property the dual-pass witnesses is what the framework names *demonstrable equivalence*: two independent discharge mechanisms operating on the same proof obligation produce identical certificates, and the agreement is the trust-conferring event. The property is demonstrated, not assumed; the framework computes both routes on every compilation and the agreement is the verification artifact. Demonstrable equivalence has four mutually reinforcing mathematical readings, each cited from documents that already contain them. The categorical reading is the cellular-sheaf compositionality equation D(s_0 < s_2) = D(s_0 < s_1) ; D(s_1 < s_2): two routes between the same endpoints of the compilation poset must produce the same answer (`the-compilation-sheaf.md`). The sheaf-theoretic reading is global section uniqueness: two valid computations of the same global section must agree pointwise (`a-triangle-without-mystery.md`). The parametricity reading is Reynolds' abstraction theorem applied at the proof layer: two proof systems applied to the same obligation produce equivalent verdicts because the obligation's meaning is preserved under parametric reasoning (`proofs-from-dimensional-types.md` and the forthcoming "Contour of the Flat Pointer"). The Hoare-logical reading is the consequence rule: two derivations of the same triple are demonstrably equivalent because they prove the same theorem (`decidability-sweet-spot.md`). These are not alternatives. They are the same structural property viewed through four mathematical lenses, each reinforcing the others. The operational mechanism by which the framework produces and reconciles the two passes' proofs at compile time to establish the agreement record (the carry-through of obligations as MLIR attributes, the role of the SMT dialect at each lowering pass, and the reconciliation step that establishes the equivalence) is subject to pending patent applications and is referenced as such in the design documentation, not described at the level of attribute schemas, dialect operations, or reconciliation algorithms. The structural property is the public commitment; the operational mechanism is the protected technique. The proof-architecture `_index.md` follows this convention.

A note on what is and is not happening at verification time. Demonstrable equivalence is established once, at compile time, by the framework's pipeline. The agreement record is materialized as part of the certificate. At verification time, the user does not re-run any solver, does not replay any proof, and does not invoke any framework-specific tool. Verification is a single Rocq kernel type-check against the certificate, which is described in the next section.

**Three architectural properties.** The connective-tissue architecture buys three properties the `_index.md` should articulate as success criteria. First, **extensibility**: new Tier 1 theories add without reworking the existing ones; specifying a witness, a refinement rule, and a decision procedure is sufficient. Second, **decidability preserved under combination**: each theory retains its own complexity bound; the total cost of Tier 1 verification grows linearly with the number of theories (the sum of the individual theories' costs, not a product) because no combined decision procedure is required. Third, **composition without unification**: the claim that Tier 1 proofs compose is true and is distinct from the claim that Tier 1 is a single logic. Composition is architectural, carried by the shared hypergraph and the consequence-rule transport at tier boundaries. No combined decision procedure is required, and each theory remains reasonable in its native terms.

### Cross-Cutting Architectural Commitments

Two architectural commitments operate at the system level rather than at the Tier 1 cohere level, and they are what give the framework's verification chain its full reach. The proof-architecture `_index.md` references both because they shape what the tier documents can claim about runtime behavior. The first is most fully articulated in the proof-architecture sub-section itself; the second is most fully articulated in the substrate-tcb sub-section.

**Deterministic Memory Management as precondition for runtime-meaningful verification.** Verification of a deployed binary's runtime behavior requires that the verifier and the runtime agree on what the program is. Deterministic Memory Management produces that agreement by committing the framework to a memory model where every value's lifetime, allocation strategy, placement, and access pattern is determined at compile time. The runtime executes within the memory layout the compiler produced, and that layout is fixed for the binary's lifetime. There is no runtime allocator making decisions; there is no collector moving live objects; there is no reference-count cycle running on its own schedule. The verification claims this enables include flat-pointer-state correctness at every dereference (the buffer's runtime address is the address the verifier reasoned about), resource bounds with precise placement, IPC buffer stability across the transmission window (the sender's invariants and the receiver's invariants are the same invariants), termination claims free of memory-management pauses, and relational claims free of memory-induced observable variation. A memory model in which any of these is decided at runtime introduces a gap between the verifier's model and the runtime's behavior, and the verification claims become source-level theorems with limited carry-through to the deployed binary. DMM closes that gap by removing the runtime decisions. The property is precondition rather than feature: it is what the framework's verification claims structurally require to mean what they say. The argument is structural and applies to any non-deterministic memory model, not specifically to garbage collection or any other particular runtime mechanism. The DTS-DMM paper (`arXiv:2603.16437`) is the formal foundation, and `decidability-sweet-spot.md` plus `memory-coeffect-algebra.md` are the implementation walkthrough.

**BAREWire as the typed wire layer that preserves invariants across substrate boundaries.** The framework's verification chain reaches across substrate boundaries through BAREWire, a typed binary protocol whose schema travels with each payload as a verification artifact in its own right. Within a Fidelity-compiled binary on any substrate, the verified invariants are properties of typed values flowing through the program. Across a BAREWire boundary, the invariants are encoded in the schema and the schema is itself a typed object in the PHG. Each theory's sheaf checks its own coherence condition against the shared schema at the transfer hyperedge: the dimensional sheaf verifies units, the representation sheaf verifies fidelity bounds, the state sheaf verifies pointer-transport mode, and the schema validation at deserialization is itself a verified operation. When the receiving side is a Fidelity-compiled binary, the schema validation recovers the invariants directly. When the receiving side is an untyped host (the JavaScript runtime is the worst-case example), the schema validation at the BAREWire boundary still occurs, and the invariants are preserved across the host's interlude in the sense that they are recovered when the payload re-enters a typed context through another BAREWire boundary. The honest framing is therefore not "BAREWire makes the untyped host verified" but "BAREWire preserves invariants across the host's interlude." The fuller treatment of this property and its substrate-by-substrate consequences lives in `substrate-tcb/_index.md`. The operational specifics of BAREWire's schema format and validation mechanism are referenced rather than detailed in the design documentation, in the same convention applied to demonstrable equivalence: the structural property is the public commitment; the operational mechanism is the protected technique.

**Three TCBs at three time points.** The framework's verification chain is planned to operate at three distinct times, each with a distinct TCB. The documentation must keep them distinct because conflating them is the most common confusion a careful reader can encounter.

At design-time, Lattice (the LSP) is to call a single SMT solver for inference and IDE feedback. The design-time TCB will be the chosen solver plus the LSP code. Rocq is not to be invoked at design-time. Coq tactics are not to be invoked at design-time. The verdicts the LSP produces will be engineer-facing only and will not enter the certificate.

At compile-time, Composer is to run the Verif/SMT dialect lowering using the same SMT solver as design-time, and Composer is also to run the Clef-to-Coq translator pass that emits Coq source as text. The compile-time TCB will be the SMT solver plus Composer plus `coqc` (which the build pipeline will invoke to compile the emitted Coq source against the framework's shipped Coq prelude). The translator is not to invoke Rocq; it is to emit text. The translator is not to invoke an SMT solver; it is to walk the framework's internal obligation representation. `coqc` is a build-system step, not a Composer-internal call, and is to consume the emitted source against the prelude.

At verification-time, a verifier is to run `rocq check` against the `.vo` certificate. The verification-time TCB will be exactly Rocq's kernel, SHA-256, the framework-shipped Coq prelude, and the Coq standard library to the extent the prelude uses it. The SMT solver from design-time and compile-time is not to be in this TCB. Composer is not to be in this TCB. The translator is not to be in this TCB. Rocq is to appear only here.

The three TCBs are designed to be minimized at their respective time points: design-time is bounded by IDE responsiveness, compile-time is bounded by build throughput, verification-time is bounded by the trust required to make the certificate dispositive. The verification-time TCB is the one that matters most for the framework's structural claim and is intended to be the smallest of the three.

**Same-solver discipline across design-time and compile-time.** The design-time and compile-time pipelines are to use the same SMT solver. The choice is between Z3 (most mature MLIR/CIRCT integration), CVC5 (open-source parity with Z3 for most fragments), and Alt-Ergo (OCaml-based, ML-family aligned). The choice will be a compile-time configuration of the framework distribution; switching solvers will require a framework rebuild but no IR changes. The same-solver requirement has three engineering justifications: the dual-pass demonstrable-equivalence reconciliation is to operate on solver verdicts at both stages, and verdicts will agree more cleanly when both stages share the same solver behavior; build packaging will be simpler with one solver integrated and tested as a unit; the patent-protected reconciliation mechanism will be simpler when there is a single solver behavior to reason about. The same-solver discipline is intended to keep the design-time and compile-time TCBs aligned and to minimize the surface area for solver-induced inconsistencies. The verification-time TCB is to be independent of the solver choice because the certificate is to be pure Coq and the prelude is to provide the discharge tactics; the solver choice will be a compile-time configuration that does not propagate to the verification side.

**The obligation hash tether (H_O).** The framework's compilation pipeline is to produce two parallel emissions from the PSG/PHG witness set: `verif.contract` operations attached to MLIR ops for compile-time discharge via Verif/SMT, and Coq source for the certificate-generation pipeline via the Clef-to-Coq translator. Both emissions are to be siblings of the same internal source. The framework is to bind them with the obligation hash, H_O, computed once at the moment Composer finalizes the PSG/PHG witnesses for emission. H_O is to be the SHA-256 hash of a canonical serialization of the obligation set, computed deterministically over the witness data so two PSG/PHG states with identical obligations will produce identical H_O regardless of node ordering or framework-internal variation.

Three artifacts are to incorporate H_O. The MLIR module is to carry it as an attribute (on the top-level module or on each `verif.formal` block, depending on what is most natural in the dialect). The compiled binary is to embed it in a non-executable section: an ELF note for CPU, an analogous metadata block for FPGA bitstreams, GPU kernels, NPU configurations, and JS modules. The `.vo` certificate is to state it as a literal in the theorem alongside the binary content hash.

At verification time, the verifier is to confirm three properties. The binary's SHA-256 content hash will match the certificate's `binary_hash` literal, which is artifact-content agreement. The H_O extracted from the binary's embedded section will match the certificate's `obligation_hash` literal, which is obligation-set agreement. The Rocq kernel will accept the certificate via `rocq check`, which is Coq proof validity. All three properties must hold for verification to succeed. Any one of them failing is to surface a specific class of integrity failure: binary-content tampering, obligation-set drift between emissions, or invalid proof.

The H_O mechanism is what is to give the dual-emission story its integrity property at the structural level. The two emissions will not be able to drift from each other without surfacing a hash mismatch at compile time (Composer-internal check between the H_O attribute on the MLIR module and the H_O literal in the emitted Coq source) or at verification time (binary-embedded H_O versus certificate-literal H_O comparison). The canonical serialization that is to produce H_O is framework-internal engineering with a one-time design effort; SHA-256 is standard. The mechanism is structural and is one of the architectural commitments that is intended to distinguish the framework's verification story from approaches that emit verification artifacts without binding them cryptographically to the program they are about. The cryptographic binding is the patent-relevant component; the operational details of the canonical serialization and the per-substrate embedding mechanism are subject to pending patent applications and are referenced rather than detailed in the design documentation.

### The Verif and SMT Dialect Architecture

The framework's compile-time obligation discharge is to use MLIR's Verif and SMT dialects, originally developed in CIRCT and being upstreamed to MLIR. The Verif dialect is to be the IR-level home for the framework's compile-time contracts; the SMT dialect is to be the encoding layer that Verif lowers to when verification flow runs. The framework's existing Hoare-logic vocabulary maps onto Verif operations directly: a Tier 1 dimensional consistency check will become a `verif.contract` with `verif.ensure` of dimensional equality; a Tier 2 bounded range check will become a `verif.contract` with `verif.require` for input bounds and `verif.ensure` for output bounds; a Tier 4 relational claim will become a `verif.formal` block wrapping a coupled computation. The framework's PSG and PHG witnesses are to be emitted as `verif.contract` operations on the corresponding MLIR ops.

Outside the verification flow, `verif.contract` is pass-through by dialect design and the contract is to have no compilation overhead. When the verification flow is active (which the framework's certificate-generation pipeline is to trigger), a sequence of standard MLIR passes will perform the discharge: `verif.contract` is to be inlined into `verif.formal` blocks, `ensure` clauses are to become `assert`s, the result is to be lowered to the SMT dialect, and SMT is to export to SMT-LIB2 (or, for in-process solving, CIRCT's JIT lowering to Z3 invokes Z3 directly without the textual round-trip).

The architectural advantage is that the framework's compile-time obligation discharge is to be built on an upstream MLIR ecosystem rather than a framework-specific encoding. The Verif and SMT dialects are being upstreamed as of the [March 2025 RFC](https://discourse.llvm.org/t/rfc-upstreaming-circts-verif-and-smt-dialects/85299), with SMT first and Verif second. The framework's plan is to target the upstream versions when available and the CIRCT versions as a transitional fallback. The translation-validation use case (Alive2-style verification of MLIR pass correctness) is explicitly in scope for these dialects and is directly relevant to the framework's self-hosting and self-verification long arc: the framework's lowering passes will be translation-validated via Verif/SMT, which is to give per-pass correctness checks at every compilation as a structural property of the Verif/SMT discharge. This is the operational mechanism for the long-arc compiler self-verification arc, not a separate proof-engineering workstream.

The solver behind the SMT dialect is solver-agnostic by design. The SMT-LIB2 export keeps every compatible solver in scope, and CIRCT's JIT lowering provides Z3 as the path of least resistance for in-process solving. The framework will pick a single solver under the same-solver discipline (Z3 via the JIT lowering, CVC5 or Alt-Ergo via SMT-LIB2 export) and use it consistently for both the design-time IDE path and the compile-time Verif/SMT lowering. The choice will be a compile-time configuration decision. The solver's verdict is to feed into the dual-pass demonstrable-equivalence reconciliation; the solver is to be in the compile-time TCB; the solver is not to be in the verification-time TCB because the certificate is to use Coq native procedures over the same obligations expressed in Coq syntax, independent of the Verif/SMT dialect work.

The relationship between Verif/SMT and the certificate-generation pipeline is sibling, not sequential. From the framework's internal obligation representation (the witnesses on PSG and PHG nodes), two emissions happen in parallel. The first emission is `verif.contract` operations into the MLIR IR, which the Verif/SMT pipeline discharges at compile time. The second emission is Coq source for the certificate-generation pipeline, which produces the proof terms that ship in the `.vo`. The two emissions target the same logical content from the same internal source, are bound to that source by the obligation hash H_O documented in Cross-Cutting Architectural Commitments, and are reconciled by the dual-pass demonstrable-equivalence mechanism. The Verif/SMT work is the compile-time correctness pass; the Coq emission is the verification-time trust artifact.

### Universal Verif/SMT Application Across Substrates

The framework's commitment to Verif/SMT as the universal compile-time discharge mechanism across every substrate (CPU, GPU, FPGA, NPU, JSIR, quantum) is novel relative to the CIRCT origin and the upstream RFC's named use cases. The CIRCT origin is hardware-focused. The upstream RFC names GPU buffer instruction optimization, tensor placement, and translation validation as upstream-relevant use cases, but treats them as episodic applications. The framework deploys Verif/SMT systematically as the canonical compile-time verification surface for every obligation across every substrate. This is a more thorough deployment than the RFC describes and is one of the architectural commitments that distinguishes the framework.

The novelty has five distinct dimensions, each individually defensible.

**Universal application across all compute substrates.** The framework treats Verif/SMT as the canonical compile-time verification surface, not as one tool among many or as a hardware-specific tool repurposed. The PSG/PHG produces obligations that are target-agnostic at the obligation level (a dimensional consistency claim is a dimensional consistency claim regardless of which substrate the binary targets), and Verif/SMT is the natural compile-time lowering target for these obligations. The substrate-tcb sub-section already records that the verification-time TCB is uniform across substrates; the universal Verif/SMT application is the compile-time analog at the dialect level.

**Integration with a tiered obligation model.** The framework's Tier 1 through Tier 4 obligations all flow through Verif/SMT at compile time, with each tier's specific decidability fragment driving the SMT formula shape. Tier 1 obligations are decidable QF_LIA and finite-lattice formulae; Tier 4 obligations involve the pRHL machinery and richer structures. The framework's tier-aware Verif emission is novel as a coordinated obligation framework over Verif/SMT, distinct from the episodic uses the RFC names.

**Cryptographic binding to the Coq certificate via the obligation hash H_O.** The Verif/SMT compile-time work and the Coq certificate are siblings of the PSG/PHG, and H_O baked into the MLIR attribute, the binary section, and the certificate literal makes the sibling relationship verifiable at verification time. This binding is custom to the framework and is what gives the dual-emission story its integrity property.

**Dual-pass demonstrable equivalence with same-solver discipline.** Using one SMT solver across both the design-time IDE path and the compile-time Verif/SMT path, with the patent-protected reconciliation establishing verdict agreement, is a novel coordination of the two passes that the standard Verif/SMT use does not have to contend with.

**PSG/PHG as the unified source of obligations.** The Verif contract operations the framework emits are not hand-written by the engineer; they are derived from the PSG/PHG witness model that Composer maintains throughout compilation. The framework's witness model is what drives Verif contract emission. The standard Verif/SMT use assumes a user writes contracts and the dialect machinery discharges them; the framework's architecture has the contracts emerge automatically from the type system and witness model. This is upstream of the use the RFC describes.

A few engineering considerations to keep on the radar without treating them as architectural conflicts. The Verif dialect's upstreaming will leave hardware-specific operations in CIRCT (`verif.clocked_assert`, `verif.format_verilog_string`, others); the framework commits only to the substrate-agnostic subset that lands upstream. The MLIR SMT dialect's solver integration is currently optimized for Z3 via CIRCT's JIT lowering and for SMT-LIB2 export, which means the framework's solver choice (per the Same-Solver Discipline) has a build-complexity dimension: Z3 via JIT is fastest for in-process solving; CVC5 or Alt-Ergo via SMT-LIB2 export is the path for ML-family alignment. The H_O attribute the framework attaches to Verif/SMT operations is a custom MLIR attribute and is bounded dialect-extension work. The translation-validation use case the RFC names aligns with the framework's compiler self-verification long arc, which compounds the value of the universal application.

### Certificate Format and Verification Mechanics

The certificate is to be a Coq compiled object (`.vo` file) emitted as a compilation byproduct, alongside the binary and the MLIR intermediates. The format is to be the same `.vo` format that ships with every Coq library distributed today. It is not to be a custom binary, not a JSON document, and not a wrapper around an SMT proof. The framework is to write no custom serializer for the certificate, and the verifier is to write no custom parser. Rocq's kernel reads `.vo` files natively because that is the format Rocq itself produces and consumes; the framework's plan is to emit into this existing format.

The verification tool is to be `rocq check` (the modern Rocq toolchain) or `coqchk` (the legacy kernel-only binary; both refer to the same kernel-level checking operation). The verifier is to invoke three commands:

```
$ rocq check mybinary.cert.vo
$ sha256sum mybinary
$ fidelity-extract-ho mybinary
```

If `rocq check` returns success, the printed `sha256sum` matches the binary content hash literal embedded in the certificate, and the printed H_O matches the obligation hash literal embedded in the certificate, the binary is to be considered verified. The third tool, `fidelity-extract-ho`, is to be a small framework-provided utility that reads the substrate-specific embedded section (an ELF note for CPU, an analogous metadata block for FPGA, GPU, and other substrates) and prints the embedded H_O. The utility is bounded engineering and its source is intended to be auditable; for an ELF binary, the same extraction will be performable with `objcopy` or `llvm-objcopy` plus shell pipes, so the utility is to be convenience rather than an additional trust dependency. Verification runtime is anticipated to be seconds for ordinary workloads (this is a design target, not a measured property). The plan does not include a JSON parser, an SMT replay step, or a framework-specific verification binary beyond the small extraction utility. The verifier will install Rocq from its standard distribution, install the framework's Coq prelude (a framework-shipped `.vo` artifact), and run the three commands above.

The TCB at verification time is to be exactly three components: Rocq's kernel, SHA-256 (any standard implementation), and the Coq standard library to the extent the framework-shipped Coq prelude uses it. The framework is to contribute none of these. Each is independently audited by communities far larger than the framework's. The framework's contribution is to be the Coq prelude (a set of `.v` files compiled to `.vo` and shipped with the distribution) and the certificates emitted as `.vo` files; both will be inputs to the verification, not part of the verification machinery.

The certificate's term states a theorem of the form:

```coq
Theorem binary_certificate :
  binary_hash = sha256_of_binary /\
  obligation_hash = H_O_of_obligations /\
  obligations_hold cert_obligations.
```

`sha256_of_binary` is a literal SHA-256 hash of the binary baked into the term as a string of bytes. `H_O_of_obligations` is the literal H_O obligation hash documented in Cross-Cutting Architectural Commitments, also baked into the term as a string of bytes. `cert_obligations` is the structured list of obligations the certificate certifies, with one entry per Tier 1 through Tier 4 obligation the compilation produced. The proof of the theorem is a Coq proof term generated by Composer's Clef-to-Coq translator. The proof references definitions and tactics in the framework-shipped Coq prelude (the prelude's axiomatization of MLIR semantics, target machine semantics, BAREWire schema validation, the obligation types, and the discharge tactics for each obligation kind). When Rocq type-checks the term, it confirms that the obligations hold under the prelude's axiomatization, that the named binary's content hash matches, and that the named obligation hash matches. The user wrote no Coq; the translator generated the term; Rocq checks it against the shipped prelude.

The binding between binary and certificate is two-fold. The first binding is the SHA-256 content hash: `sha256sum mybinary` must match the certificate's `binary_hash` literal. The second binding is the H_O obligation hash: the H_O extracted from the binary's substrate-specific embedded section (an ELF note for CPU, an analogous metadata block for FPGA, GPU, and other substrates) must match the certificate's `obligation_hash` literal. Both bindings are checked by the verifier after Rocq's kernel has type-checked the certificate. If the binary is modified after emission, the content hash mismatches. If the obligation set drifted between the dual emissions, the H_O hashes mismatch. If the certificate is modified after emission, Rocq's kernel rejects the modified `.vo` (Coq compiled objects carry structural integrity checks built into the format; a modified `.vo` either fails to load or fails to type-check). The three-way integrity (content hash, obligation hash, kernel acceptance) is sufficient to detect tampering at any link in the chain.

For obligations in decidable theories (Tier 1 dimensional, Tier 1 flat-pointer, Tier 1 grade, Tier 1 numeric representation, Tier 2 bounded, Tier 3 distributional), the framework's certificate-generation pipeline uses Coq's native reflective decision procedures exclusively, wrapped in tactics provided by the framework's Coq prelude. No external SMT solver is in the certificate-generation pipeline. The verification-time TCB is exactly Rocq's kernel, SHA-256, the Coq standard library to the extent the framework's Coq prelude uses it, and the framework's Coq prelude itself. Nothing is added by the certificate-generation strategy.

Coq's native procedures cover the Tier 1 obligations directly. `lia` (linear integer arithmetic, exactly QF_LIA, OCaml-implemented as part of Coq itself) discharges dimensional consistency over `Z^n` and grade preservation in the abelian-group framework. The finite-lattice procedures from the Coq standard library and the Mathematical Components library handle flat-pointer state-set inclusion and lattice intersection. For Tier 1 numeric representation, the obligations the framework actually emits are structural claims about which representation is selected given the dimensional type (which posit width, which IEEE-754 format, which fixed-point layout). These fit cleanly in Coq's native machinery without requiring external bitvector solvers; the hard QF_BV cases (cryptographic bit-manipulation, hash function round transformations) live at Tier 4 by design and are addressed by tactics in the framework's Coq prelude that correspond to primitives in the Clef library. For Tier 2 bounded ranges, `lia` and `lra` discharge most claims; the residual cases use prelude tactics. For Tier 3 distributional obligations, the prelude provides tactics for the distributional fragment that the certificate's translated form references. For Tier 4, the prelude provides the pRHL machinery. In all cases, the prelude is framework-shipped and the per-compilation Coq is generated by the translator.

The framework's certificate-generation pipeline therefore writes no bridge to an external SMT solver and adds no non-ML-family component to the verification-time TCB. This is the strongest TCB minimization the framework can adopt and aligns the entire trust chain with the ML-family tradition (Coq is implemented in OCaml; `lia` and the other reflective procedures are OCaml-implemented Coq plugins; the framework's Coq prelude is `.v` source compiled to `.vo` and shipped with the framework distribution).

The design-time pipeline calls a single SMT solver for inference and IDE feedback. Rocq is not at design-time. The LSP does not invoke Coq tactics. The solver choice is between Z3 (most mature MLIR/CIRCT integration), CVC5 (open-source parity with Z3 for most fragments), and Alt-Ergo (OCaml-based, ML-family aligned). The same solver is used at compile-time by the Verif/SMT dialect lowering for engineering consistency: dual-pass verdicts agree on the same obligations more cleanly when both passes share a solver, build packaging is simpler with one solver integrated, and the patent-protected reconciliation mechanism is simpler when both passes have a single solver behavior to reason about. The solver choice is a compile-time configuration; it is in the design-time and compile-time TCBs but not in the verification-time TCB, because the certificate-generation pipeline emits Coq source independently and the Coq side never invokes the SMT solver.

The trade-off the framework absorbs is more work in the Clef library `Fidelity.Lemmas.Mathematics` and in the framework-shipped Coq prelude. The Clef library grows with the framework's verification capability and is written in Clef; the Coq prelude grows with each framework release and is shipped as a fixed artifact, not authored per program. Where an external SMT solver might have automated certain Tier 2 or Tier 3 obligations, the framework now adds the corresponding primitive to the Clef library and the corresponding tactic to the Coq prelude, both as framework-internal engineering. The growth is the canonical form of the framework's verification capability, expressed in Clef on the user-facing side and in the prelude on the verification-side. This is consistent with the canonization-as-admission-criterion architecture and is the engineering-side counterpart to the framework's structural answer to Mathslop: canonization is the witness, the witness lives in Clef (user-facing) and in the framework-shipped Coq prelude (verification-side), and the trust chain stays inside Coq's kernel for everything the certificate asserts.

For Tier 4 obligations, the certificate references prelude-defined lemmas applied to Clef-derived data. The prelude is distributed alongside the framework as a set of `.v` files compiled to `.vo` at framework build time. Rocq has already type-checked every prelude lemma when the prelude was built. The certificate's references to the prelude are resolved by `rocq check` at verification time. The Clef library `Fidelity.Lemmas.Mathematics` is processed by the Clef-to-Coq translator alongside user code; its Coq emission is generated, not hand-authored. If the prelude has changed since the certificate was emitted (different commit hash, modified tactic, modified definition), the certificate's references resolve to the wrong terms and verification fails. If the Clef library has changed, the regenerated Coq emission may differ from the certificate's content, and verification fails for the same structural reason.

The framework's integration with Rocq is at the source-text level. The Composer compiler emits `.v` files (Coq source) as a compilation byproduct from the Clef-to-Coq translator pass. The build pipeline invokes `coqc` (the standard Coq compiler binary) to produce the `.vo` certificate from the emitted `.v` source against the framework's Coq prelude. This is the same integration pattern CompCert uses and the same pattern the verified-software community has used for decades. The framework writes no Coq plugin, maintains no binding to Coq's OCaml internals, and ships no custom serializer. The integration is text generation by the translator plus a build-system invocation of a standard Coq tool. The user never opens a `.v` file or writes a Coq tactic; the entire user-facing surface is Clef.

A future optimization is a tighter binding analogous to the role Farscape plays for C and C++ bindings. Coq exposes programmatic interfaces (the SerAPI / coq-lsp protocol over standard IO, and Coq's OCaml plugin API for in-process integration) that allow external tools to construct Coq terms and emit `.vo` files without going through `coqc` source compilation. A Farscape-style Rocq binding for Composer would let the compiler emit `.vo` directly from its compilation pipeline, avoiding the per-build `coqc` invocation cost and the source-text serialization round-trip. The optimization is real but is not required for shipping verified software. CompCert ships verified software using the source-text + `coqc` path. The framework should adopt the same path initially and consider the tighter binding when compilation throughput becomes the binding constraint, not before. The Farscape-style binding is recorded here as a known future optimization, not as a phase-one requirement.

The patent boundary applies as elsewhere. The structural commitment (certificate is a `.vo`, verification is `rocq check`, TCB at verification time is Rocq's kernel plus SHA-256 plus the framework-shipped Coq prelude plus the Coq standard library, framework integration is Clef-to-Coq text generation plus `coqc` invocation) is public and standard. The internal mechanism by which the compilation pipeline produces the `.vo` (the dual-pass demonstrable-equivalence reconciliation, the canonical serialization that produces H_O, the carry-through of obligations as MLIR attributes, and the per-substrate H_O embedding mechanism) is patent material and is referenced in the documentation, not specified at the level of attribute schemas, dialect operations, serialization algorithms, or reconciliation procedures.

### PHG Emergence and Multi-Theory Joint Constraints

The Program Hypergraph emerges from typing rules during elaboration, not from a top-down structural commitment. Each operation's typing rule determines the shape of the hyperedge that represents it, and the hypergraph grows as the PSG is built. This is the framing the "Fabric of Tier 1" article makes explicit and the framing the proof-architecture `_index.md` should adopt.

The PHG is most often introduced through the Clifford-algebra grade system and the Cayley-table elimination story for AI workloads, but the PHG is already structurally required at Tier 1 itself, before any of those applications enter the picture. Two distinct forces drive its emergence at Tier 1.

The first force is the natural arity of operations. A binary arithmetic operation creates a 2-source hyperedge. A k-simplex join creates a (k+1)-source hyperedge. A tile co-location constraint creates a hyperedge whose source set is the full set of co-located nodes. An array indexing operation creates a hyperedge whose source positions hold, jointly, the array pointer's flat pointer state, the element type's dimensional annotation, the index value's range information, and the resulting value's combined type. The hyperedge captures the operation's joint relational semantics; a binary-edge representation would scatter the joint relation across separate edges with intermediate nodes that carry no semantic content, forcing the compiler to reconstruct the relation from its parts whenever it needed to reason about the operation.

The second force is the joint constraint across multiple Tier 1 theories. When the validity of an operation requires the dimensional fiber, the state fiber, and the representation fiber to agree simultaneously across three or more nodes, the joint validity is not decomposable into pairwise edges that preserve the agreement. A fused gradient-update step that has to satisfy a dimensional invariant over `Z^n`, a flat pointer state invariant over the finite lattice (the gradient buffer must be in the `valid` state, not `nil` or `forwarded`), and a representation invariant over QF_BV (the posit width must be sufficient for the accumulation discipline) all simultaneously across three operations is the simplest realistic example. The hyperedge is the minimum structure that holds the joint constraint, and a binary-edge representation would admit annotation assignments that the actual hardware cannot honor.

These two forces are complementary, not alternative. The first is about operation arity (the operation itself has multiple semantic positions); the second is about theory composition (multiple theories must agree on the same edge). The PHG accommodates both because hyperedges are arity-parametric and because each theory contributes its own fiber over the same base. Geometric-algebra grade preservation, spatial dataflow joint constraints on FPGAs and NPUs, and the quantum-circuit constraints the quantum substrate covers are all dramatic instances of the second force; they are not its origin. The origin is at the boundary between the multiple Tier 1 theories the PSG carries simultaneously, and the PHG documentation should reflect both forces equally.

The proof-architecture sub-section is therefore structured to introduce the PHG as a Tier 1 phenomenon first, with the Clifford, spatial, and quantum applications named as instances of the multi-theory joint-constraint pattern. The compilation-sheaf document already reads this way at the categorical level. The new sub-section makes the engineering version of the same reading the canonical statement, with the parallel-fibers connective tissue from the previous section as the framing that gives the joint-constraint story its proper architectural shape.

The Verif/SMT discharge is the compile-time correctness pass; the Coq emission is the verification-time trust artifact. Rocq is at verification-time only.

The document also records the translation-validation pathway as the operational mechanism for the framework's compiler self-verification long arc. The Verif/SMT dialects support Alive2-style translation validation of MLIR passes, which gives per-pass correctness checks at every compilation. For the framework's Tier 4 compiler-self-verification work, this is the path: each lowering pass takes input MLIR and produces output MLIR; the equivalence claim is expressed as a `verif.formal` block; the discharge happens via the same SMT solver the framework uses elsewhere; the per-pass certificates compose into the framework's compiler-correctness certificate. The Clef-to-Coq translator emits the Coq counterpart for the certificate, and the framework-shipped Coq prelude provides the discharge tactics. This is the engineering version of CompCert's compiler-correctness story, with the labor distribution shifted away from hand-written tactics toward the translation-validation infrastructure that the Verif/SMT dialects provide.

The document records the framework's commitment to ML-family alignment for the entire verification-time trust chain (Rocq's kernel plus SHA-256 plus the Coq standard library plus the framework-shipped Coq prelude). For the design-time and compile-time TCBs the framework picks a single SMT solver under the same-solver discipline; the choice favors ML-family alignment when feasible (Alt-Ergo) and accepts well-integrated alternatives (Z3, CVC5) when engineering constraints require them.

For Tier 4 obligations, the document covers the prelude-tactic reference pattern: certificates apply tactics from the framework-shipped Coq prelude to data derived from the Clef library `Fidelity.Lemmas.Mathematics` (which is itself Clef code processed by the Clef-to-Coq translator). The prelude is distributed as compiled `.vo` files alongside the framework. `rocq check` resolves the certificate's tactic applications against the prelude at verification time. The Clef library is the source of the framework's mathematical primitives; the prelude is the source of the discharge machinery; the user writes neither, the framework ships both.

It is the design document a verifier reads before running `rocq check`, and it is the document that records the Farscape-style Rocq binding when that work is done. It cites the SpeakEZ Dangers of Unearned Press post for the structural argument that the verification-time TCB must be small to be credible, and cross-references the substrate-tcb sub-section for substrate-specific verification reach.

The document also records the engineering workstreams the certificate-generation pipeline requires the framework to build. There is no latent capability in TableGen, MLIR, or the surrounding Cpp toolchain that emits Coq source from a framework-defined IR; this work is owned by the framework. The architectural commitment is that the user writes Clef and only Clef. The Coq side is generated, not hand-authored. There are three workstreams, all framework-internal.

The first workstream is a **fixed Coq prelude shipped with the framework distribution**. The prelude defines the obligation types in Coq syntax (dimensional vectors over `Z^n`, the flat-pointer state lattice, grade structures, range-bounds primitives, the pRHL machinery for Tier 4), provides the tactics that discharge each obligation kind (`lia` and reflective procedures wrapped in framework-friendly tactics), and provides the certificate validator (`valid_certificate : Certificate -> Prop`) and apply-tactic (`apply_certificate`) that let Coq's kernel type-check a per-compilation manifest in a single pass. The prelude is one-time engineering with version maintenance as the framework's vocabulary evolves. It is exactly analogous to a runtime library shipped with a compiler, except its consumer is Coq's kernel rather than a CPU. The prelude does not grow per user program; it grows per framework release. The prelude is the only Coq artifact the framework ships, and it is shipped, not written per program.

The second workstream is the **Clef-to-Coq translator in Composer**. This is a compilation pipeline pass that walks Clef source, identifies obligations from Clef's type system at PSG and PHG nodes, and emits Coq source that references the prelude's vocabulary and applies the prelude's tactics. The translator is structurally similar to Composer's MLIR emission but targets Coq syntax instead of MLIR. The output is a Coq source file containing a structured manifest of obligations and tactic applications, compiled by `coqc` to a `.vo`. The translator is bounded compiler engineering; the difficulty is in the systematic mapping from Clef's type-level obligations to Coq syntactic forms that the prelude's tactics can discharge, not in inventing new proof technology. The framework writes this translator the same way it writes its MLIR emitter: as a code-generation pass over the framework's own internal representation.

The third workstream is the **Clef library `Fidelity.Lemmas.Mathematics`**, which is **Clef code, not Coq code**. It contains the framework's mathematical primitives (dimensional algebra operations, geometric algebra grade operations, posit arithmetic primitives, range-bounds combinators, anything else the framework's verification capability rests on) written in Clef syntax. The library is consumed by user programs as ordinary Clef imports. The Clef-to-Coq translator processes the library the same way it processes any other Clef code, emitting Coq source for verification purposes. The user does not write Coq when extending this library; the user writes Clef. The library grows with the framework's verification capability, and its growth is the canonical form of that capability, but the growth is in Clef.

The user's workflow is straightforward: write Clef. The compilation pipeline does the rest. Composer compiles Clef to MLIR for the binary, emits `verif.contract` operations into the IR for compile-time obligation discharge via Verif/SMT, and emits Coq source via the Clef-to-Coq translator. `coqc` compiles the emitted Coq source against the prelude to produce a `.vo`. The `.vo` is the certificate. The user never opens a `.v` file, never writes a tactic, never reads Coq syntax. The Coq side is a generated artifact, not a development target.

The gating workstream is the first (the fixed Coq prelude), because nothing else can be tested until the prelude exists. The second (the translator) is bounded compiler engineering that depends on the prelude. The third (the Clef library) is the long-arc verification capability investment, written in Clef alongside the rest of the framework's code. The framework's roadmap should sequence these explicitly, with the prelude landing first.

## Sub-Section Two: Substrate TCB

Path: `/docs/design/substrate-tcb/`.

### TCB Dimensions Covered

Each substrate document covers four trust dimensions. Naming them up front makes the documents comparable.

The **toolchain TCB** is the set of compilation tools whose correctness must be assumed. This includes the LLVM backend, the substrate-specific MLIR dialects and lowerings, and any third-party synthesis tool downstream of MLIR.

The **runtime TCB** is the set of runtime components whose correctness must be assumed. This includes drivers, kernels, virtual machines, language runtimes, and any process-level service the binary depends on.

The **hardware TCB** is the set of hardware behaviors whose correctness must be assumed. This includes the ISA contract, microarchitectural assumptions (cache coherence, memory ordering), and any vendor-specific extensions the binary uses.

The **attestation chain** is the set of mechanisms by which the deployed binary's compliance with the source-level specification can be re-verified after deployment. This includes the verification certificate, the reconciliation tool, and any BAREWire-mediated boundary that extends invariants beyond the compiled binary.

### Sub-Section Structure

`substrate-tcb/_index.md` carries the cross-substrate verified-substrate claim as the sub-section's central thesis, with the per-substrate documents framed as supporting evidence and the mechanical demonstrations as the proof-points the claim requires.

The thesis. The framework's TCB reach is the consequence of a specific combination of architectural commitments: compilation-intrinsic verification (no extraction gap, as articulated in the Dangers of Unearned Press post); Deterministic Memory Management (no runtime non-determinism, as cross-referenced from the proof-architecture sub-section's Cross-Cutting Architectural Commitments); multi-substrate compilation from a single source (CPU, GPU, NPU, FPGA, JSIR, quantum from one Clef program); the universal application of the Verif/SMT dialects as the compile-time discharge mechanism across every substrate (a novel deployment relative to the CIRCT origin and the upstream RFC's named use cases); BAREWire-mediated cross-substrate invariant preservation (the typed wire layer that travels with each payload); dual-pass demonstrable equivalence with same-solver discipline (the verification artifact itself is verified, with the operational mechanism subject to pending patent applications); and the obligation hash tether H_O (a canonical SHA-256 hash of the obligation set, baked into the MLIR module, the binary, and the certificate, that cryptographically binds the dual emissions to the same parent PSG/PHG state). The combination yields a verification chain that runs from source through each substrate's compilation pipeline into the deployed binary, then across BAREWire-mediated boundaries to the next substrate, with the invariants surviving every transit and verifiable at any point against the certificate via `rocq check` and the H_O comparison. No other framework offers this combination. CompCert is single-substrate (CPU). Project Everest reaches into Low* and KaRaMeL but does not span multiple compute substrates. LEAN-based extraction never reaches the binary. Rust's memory safety story is compile-time and substrate-local. The framework's TCB reach is the consequence of the combination, not of any single architectural feature, and that is the structural claim the substrate-tcb sub-section substantiates.

BAREWire as the typed wire layer. The cross-substrate claim depends on BAREWire's role at every substrate boundary. Within a Fidelity-compiled binary on any substrate, the verified invariants are properties of typed values. Across a BAREWire boundary, the invariants are encoded in the schema, which travels with the payload as a verification artifact in its own right. The transfer is represented in the PHG as a hyperedge annotated with the schema, and each theory's sheaf checks its own coherence condition against the shared schema at that hyperedge. Schema validation at deserialization is itself a verified operation. For Fidelity-side receivers the invariants are recovered directly; for untyped hosts the invariants are preserved across the host's interlude in the sense that they are recovered at the next BAREWire boundary. The honest framing is preservation, not native verification of the host. The operational specifics of BAREWire's schema format and validation mechanism follow the patent-boundary convention applied throughout the design documentation: structural claim public, operational mechanism referenced rather than detailed.

Document structure of the index. The index opens by stating the thesis and naming the four TCB dimensions (toolchain TCB, runtime TCB, hardware TCB, attestation chain). It provides an at-a-glance table mapping each substrate to its TCB profile in each dimension with brief annotations. It articulates the BAREWire-as-typed-wire framing as the structural mechanism for cross-substrate invariant preservation. It cross-references the proof-architecture sub-section's Cross-Cutting Architectural Commitments for the DMM-as-precondition argument, the three-TCBs-at-three-time-points distinction, the same-solver discipline, and the H_O obligation hash tether (each is articulated in proof-architecture but referenced from substrate-tcb). It cross-references `proof-architecture/certificate-mechanics.md` for the canonical statement of the certificate format and the verification workflow, and it names `rocq check` as the verification command that applies uniformly across all substrates: a `.vo` certificate is a `.vo` certificate regardless of which substrate the binary targets, and the verification-time TCB (Rocq's kernel, SHA-256, the framework-shipped Coq prelude, and the Coq standard library to the extent the prelude uses it) is the same across CPU, GPU, FPGA, NPU, JSIR, and quantum. What differs by substrate is the compilation-time TCB (which downstream toolchains the framework relies on to produce the binary) and the attestation chain's reach into the deployed runtime, not the verification-time TCB. The H_O comparison at verification time is also uniform across substrates: the verifier extracts H_O from the binary's substrate-specific embedded section and compares against the certificate literal regardless of whether the embedded section is an ELF note, a bitstream metadata block, a GPU kernel header, or a JS module side-channel. The index makes this distinction explicit so a reader does not conflate compile-time TCB variation with verification-time TCB variation. It closes with the explicit acknowledgment that compilation-time TCB sizes vary by orders of magnitude across substrates, that the framework's compilation-time verification reach is bounded by the smallest compilation-time TCB it can deliver to, and that the credibility of the cross-substrate claim depends on mechanical demonstrations as well as architectural assertion.

Mechanical demonstrations as the discipline. The HelloArty repository is the existing reference deployment for the FPGA substrate and demonstrates compile-time guarantees surviving into hardware. The cross-substrate claim requires similar deployments at the other substrate boundaries to be credible at the level the architectural assertion makes possible: a CPU-only Hello World with a verifiable certificate, a CPU-to-GPU pipeline with a BAREWire-mediated handoff and a verifiable round-trip, a CPU-to-FPGA pipeline (HelloArty extended with a CPU partner that exchanges data via BAREWire), and a CPU-to-JSIR pipeline (a Fidelity backend talking to a Fable-compiled JS frontend via BAREWire over WebSocket, with the schema validated at both ends). Each is a small, deployable artifact that demonstrates a specific link in the verification chain. The substrate-tcb sub-section identifies these as the proof-points the cross-substrate claim requires and lists their assembly as work the framework should plan against. Without the mechanical evidence the claim reads as architectural assertion; with it the claim reads as engineering demonstration.

The CPU document is the reference. Other substrate documents are described relative to it as supporting evidence for the thesis. The substrates with the smallest TCB exposure (CPU, FPGA via CIRCT) anchor the claim; the substrates with larger TCB exposure (GPU, NPU, JSIR, quantum) are described with the reach honestly delineated and the BAREWire mechanism named as the bridge that lets the framework still meaningfully participate at those substrates without overclaiming.

`substrate-tcb/cpu.md` is the reference document. The toolchain TCB is LLVM plus the standard ELF loader. The runtime TCB is the kernel boundary plus minimal libc. The hardware TCB is the ISA contract for the target architecture. The attestation chain extends to the binary's external `.vo` certificate and the H_O obligation hash embedded in the binary's `.note.fidelity` ELF section, with verification via `rocq check` plus `sha256sum` plus extraction of the embedded H_O for comparison against the certificate's literal. BAREWire enters when the binary participates in IPC or networked communication. This is the substrate where the framework's verification reach is largest and where most of the formal arguments in the four arXiv preprints are immediately operational. The CPU document is the reference for H_O embedding (ELF note as the canonical mechanism) and other substrate documents describe their analogous embedding mechanisms relative to it.

`substrate-tcb/gpu.md` covers the GPU substrate. The toolchain TCB adds the GPU dialect lowering and the vendor's compiler (NVCC for CUDA, ROCm's compiler stack for AMD, the SPIR-V toolchain for Vulkan compute). The runtime TCB adds the GPU driver and the runtime library (CUDA Runtime, HIP Runtime, Vulkan loader). The hardware TCB adds the GPU's ISA, its memory model, and any vendor extensions the kernel uses. The attestation chain is reduced: the verified MLIR is preserved through the GPU dialect, but the vendor compiler's transformations are outside the verification ledger. The hardware TCB is the FPGA fabric's behavioral contract under the synthesized configuration. The attestation chain reaches into the bitstream when CIRCT is used; with Vivado/Vitis, the chain ends at the place-and-route step. A consolidated file would obscure the regime structure the flat pointer article makes explicit. If document count becomes a concern at writing time, a paired structure (`tier-1-parametric.md` covering the three parametric theories and `tier-1-representation.md` covering QF_BV) is the principled fallback, but the four-file structure is the default.

**Whether the "Contour of the Flat Pointer" and "Fabric of Tier 1" blog posts are published before or after the corresponding design documents**. Either order works for either pair. The Contour post pairs with `tier-1-flat-pointer.md`; the Fabric post pairs with `proof-architecture/_index.md`. If a post lands first, the design document cites it as the prose treatment. If the design document lands first, the post is updated at publication time to link into it. The cross-link discipline is symmetric. The two posts are deliberate companion pieces dated the same day, so publishing them as a pair reads cleanly and the plan recommends that pairing.

**Whether `substrate-tcb/quantum.md` is included now or deferred**. The hardware maturity gap means the substrate is not yet operational in the same sense the others are. The plan includes it for completeness; if the document risks reading as aspirational, it can be deferred until the categorical-foundations quantum document is augmented with the necessary scoping.

**Whether the summary blog post lives on SpeakEZ or also has a Clef-side companion**. The plan recommends SpeakEZ-only; a Clef-side technical announcement at `/blog/` is optional. The Contour and Fabric posts already serve as the Clef-side prose treatment of the connective-tissue architecture, so a separate Clef-side announcement would be redundant unless it covers material the two existing posts do not.

These are decisions for the moment writing begins; they do not affect the plan's structure.
